# Wireframes / storyboards

1. Hook / Framing — “Classical Music Is for Everyone… Right?”

Classical music is often positioned as a universal art form—one that transcends culture, language, and identity. It is frequently described as accessible to anyone willing to listen. However, when we look at who actually attends classical music performances, a different picture begins to emerge. Audience demographics suggest that participation is not evenly distributed across society. This raises an important question: if classical music is truly for everyone, why don’t its audiences reflect the diversity of the broader population?

2. The Problem — “Who’s Missing from the Audience?”

A closer look at audience demographics reveals clear disparities in who is represented in classical music spaces. Compared to the overall U.S. population, orchestra audiences remain overwhelmingly white, with significantly lower representation from Black, Hispanic, and other minority communities. These patterns suggest that certain groups are consistently underrepresented, not just occasionally absent. Traditionally, this gap has often been framed as an issue of audience interest or engagement—but this explanation alone fails to fully account for the scale and consistency of the disparity.

3. Turning Point — “Maybe It’s Not the Audience”

Rather than viewing this issue as a failure of audiences to engage, it may be more useful to reconsider how classical music institutions themselves are structured. If certain communities are not showing up, it is worth asking whether they are being meaningfully represented or invited in the first place. This shift in perspective reframes the problem: instead of asking why audiences are disengaged, we begin to ask how representation within the field might influence who feels included in these spaces.

4. Representation on Stage — “Who Gets to Perform?”

When examining who is represented on stage, similar patterns of disparity emerge. Orchestra musicians, conductors, and leadership roles do not reflect the diversity of the broader population. The majority of performers and decision-makers in classical music institutions are white, with significantly lower representation from minority communities. This lack of diversity in visible and influential roles can shape how audiences perceive the art form, reinforcing the idea that classical music is tied to a narrow set of identities and experiences.

5. Representation Over Time — “Is This Changing?”

Looking at trends over time, progress toward greater representation has been limited. While there have been incremental changes in the demographic makeup of orchestras, these shifts have been relatively slow and uneven. The persistence of these patterns suggests that the issue is not temporary, but rather structural. Without significant changes to how institutions recruit, program, and engage, representation gaps are likely to continue shaping the field in the same ways.

6. Programming / Repertoire — “Whose Music Is Being Played?”

Representation in classical music extends beyond who performs and is also reflected in whose work is programmed. The vast majority of repertoire performed by orchestras is composed by white male composers, with works by women and composers of color making up only a small fraction of programming. This imbalance reinforces a limited narrative about whose voices are valued within the art form. When audiences consistently see and hear the same perspectives represented, it can further contribute to feelings of exclusion or disconnection.

7. Connecting the Dots — “Representation Shapes Engagement”

When these elements are considered together—who performs, what is programmed, and who attends—a clear relationship begins to emerge. Representation within institutions appears to shape audience engagement, not just reflect it. If the people on stage and the stories being told do not resonate with diverse audiences, it becomes less likely that those audiences will feel a sense of belonging in these spaces. This suggests that disparities in attendance are not simply about interest, but about alignment between institutions and the communities they aim to serve.

8. Conclusion — “It’s Not Who Isn’t Showing Up, It’s Who’s Not Represented”

Ultimately, this project reframes the conversation around classical music audiences. Rather than viewing underrepresentation as a failure of engagement, it highlights how institutional structures and representation shape access and participation. Expanding representation—through programming, performers, and leadership—has the potential to create more inclusive and welcoming spaces. In this way, diversity is not only an ethical goal, but a necessary step toward building more sustainable and engaged audiences for the future.

# User research 

## Target audience
The primary audience for this project is arts administrators, educators, and nonprofit professionals working within or adjacent to the classical music field, particularly those involved in programming, audience development, and community engagement. This also includes students and emerging arts leaders who are beginning to think critically about access and equity within cultural institutions. Beyond the field, I aim to reach a broader audience of individuals interested in the arts who may feel disconnected from classical music but are curious about why that disconnect exists.


## Interview script
Is the overall narrative arc clear and easy to follow?
Does the transition from audience disparities to representation feel convincing and well-supported?
Are the key takeaways from each section clear without needing additional explanation?
Does the story feel engaging and persuasive, or are there areas where it loses momentum?

The goal here was mainly to get feedback on clarity and story structure as visualizations were not nearly as fleshed out.


## Interview findings

Across peer feedback, a consistent takeaway was that the overall story structure is clear, logical, and more engaging than initially expected. Reviewers found the narrative arc—moving from the assumption of universality, to identifying audience disparities, and then reframing the issue around representation—to be easy to follow and compelling. This was especially encouraging, as I had been concerned that the essay-like structure might feel too dense or academic. Instead, feedback suggested that the progression felt natural and helped guide the viewer through the argument without confusion.

Another key insight was that while the story itself is strong, the effectiveness of the project will ultimately depend on how clearly the data is visualized. Peers emphasized the importance of simplifying charts and ensuring that each visualization communicates a single, clear takeaway. There was also helpful technical feedback around preparing data for visualization, specifically suggestions on converting summarized data points into a .csv formats to better utilize tools like Tableau.

Overall, the biggest lesson learned is that clarity and accessibility should remain the priority. A strong narrative is only as effective as its ability to be quickly understood.

# Identified changes for Part III
Based on the feedback received, I plan to focus on refining both the clarity of my visualizations and the structure of my underlying data. While the overall narrative and story flow will remain largely the same—since this was identified as a strength—I will make targeted improvements to ensure that each section is as clear and effective as possible.

First, I will begin on my data viz and ensure that each chart communicates a single, clear takeaway. This includes refining chart types where necessary, improving labeling, and establishing a consistent color scheme across all visuals. I also plan to add brief annotations or callouts to guide the viewer’s attention to key insights, making the visuals more intuitive and reducing the need for additional explanation.

Second, I will restructure my data into clean, organized .csv files to better support visualization in Tableau. I'll have to consolidate key data points from reports into structured datasets that allow for easier manipulation, comparison, and consistency across charts. Improving my data organization will help streamline the visualization process and result in more polished, accurate outputs.
