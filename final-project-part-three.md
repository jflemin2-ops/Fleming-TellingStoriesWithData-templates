# Changes made since Part II
As I continued developing the project, one of the most significant changes I made was in how I visualized representation within orchestral programming. Initially, I was relying on more straightforward comparison charts, but I realized they weren’t fully capturing the scale of imbalance so I expanded outward to include white composers as well, not just musicians and executives. Shifting to a treemap allowed me to show proportions in a way that felt much more immediate and impactful. Instead of reading percentages, the viewer can visually see how much space white composers occupy relative to others, which strengthened the clarity of my argument.

I also had to rethink the overall structure of the project. My original approach followed a more traditional essay format, which worked conceptually but didn’t translate well into the Shorthand platform. The narrative felt too linear and text-heavy for a format that is meant to be more visual and interactive. As a result, I restructured the story to be driven more by the data itself, allowing each visualization to introduce and support key ideas rather than relying on long-form written transitions. This shift made the project feel more engaging and aligned with the expectations of a data storytelling format.

Another major development came from incorporating new data from the Institute for Composer Diversity. This gave me access to more detailed programming data across seasons, which allowed me to identify a clear spike in the inclusion of Black composers during the 2021–2022 concert season. Rather than presenting this as steady progress, I was able to frame it as a reactive response following the George Floyd protests. This insight ultimately reshaped the direction of my story, moving it away from a general discussion of underrepresentation and toward a more critical look at how institutions respond to moments of social pressure.

Because of this, my call to action also evolved. Instead of broadly advocating for increased diversity, I now focus more specifically on the need for intentional and sustained programming decisions. The data suggests that short-term increases in representation are possible, but without long-term commitment, those changes risk being temporary. This reframing allowed the project to move beyond observation and toward a more actionable and grounded conclusion.
